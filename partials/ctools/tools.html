PRISMS Experimental Tools


The PRISMS center is also developing or applying a number of novel experimental methods and techniques to enable our scientific research.


SEM-DIC

The Daly group has developed (under DOE-BES Award No. DE-SC0003996 and NSF Grant No. 092753) a means for full-field, high resolution mapping of deformations at the microstructural length scale using a combination of scanning electron microscopy and digital image correlation (termed here as SEM-DIC). The Daly group created chemically functionalized nanoparticle self-assembly methods for unprecedented spatial resolution, and developed a framework originally proposed by Sutton et al. (Sutton et al., Experimental Mechanics, 2007, 2-part paper series) to correct for the complex spatial and temporal distortions inherent in SEM imaging. These techniques, combined with corrections for stress relaxation and hardware/software developments for various thermo-mechanical testing parameters, enable the acquisition of highly accurate and statistically relevant sub-grain measurements of deformation. Distortion-corrected SEM-DIC provides the accuracy, spatial resolution, and large amounts of data needed to perform quantitative characterization linking microstructure to deformation.


High Resolution Characterization

Atomic scale alloy microstructures are investigated using atom probe tomography (APT) and high angle annular dark field (HAADF) scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM). Full descriptions of the internal atomic structure, chemistry, interface structure, number density, size distributions and 3D morphologies of precipitates may be obtained from the complementarity of the two imaging and analysis techniques. To this end, robust and efficient quantification methodologies of the data are being developed.

Ultrasonic Fatigue SEM (UF-SEM) System

The Jones, Allison and Daly groups have developed a unique capability for rapidly characterizing fatigue characteristics of materials at ultrasonic frequencies. Within the PRISMS Center we are extending this capability further by inserting our ultrasonic fatigue apparatus into a scanning electron microscope. This enables us to characterize crack interactions with local microstructural features in real-time at high magnification. The system currently combines ultrasonic fatigue instrumentation with a Tescan Mira3 scanning electron microscope, shown below. The load line of the ultrasonic fatigue system is comprised of an ultrasonic converter that generates a sinusoidal displacement using a piezoelectric material stack, a Ti-6Al-4V amplification horn that magnifies the signal from the ultrasonic converter, a Ti-6Al-4V lambda rod used for adjusting the length of the load line, and the fatigue specimen to be tested. The load line is mounted in a custom built SEM chamber door, and is controlled by electronic instrumentation that maintains the ultrasonic resonant frequency of the load line to within ±1 Hz by monitoring the specimen input displacement using a piezoelectric transducer in a closed loop control system. Rotation of the load line along the longitudinal axis allows for in situ electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) scanning for crystallographic characterization of areas of interest.
